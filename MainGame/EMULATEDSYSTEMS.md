# Emulated systems
We will emulate 8 bit consoles and home computer as well as some arcade games. 
The basic idea of this game is you are playing from 1979 to 1984 and systems, games and peripherals are releasing slowly over that time.

## Consoles

### Atari 2600 / VCS
Released in 1977 September. Most catridges would also work on the Atari 400/800. 

Controllers:
- 9 pin Atari style connectors
- 8 direction joysticks
- One axis paddles
- One fire button
- Commodore and Atari home comptuers use the same contollers
- Keyboard, "kids" keyboard, and trackball were also available, but few games used them

### Odyssey 2
Released 1978 September

Controllers:
- Had a built in membrane keyboard
- 8 way joysticks
- One fire button
- Early on controllers were hard wired to the console; eventually they used a DB9 similar to Atari
- Speach unit available

### Intellivision
Released in 1979. It was mid 1980 before the system was widely available. Intellivision II was released in 1983 spring, it was just a cost reduced version with incomptability with a few games. 

Controllers:
- Came with two hard wired 12 button 16 way controllers. Games came with overlays for the buttons. 
- Expensive keyboard unit was available, but it ended up being fraught with controversy. It was discontinued in August 1982 with a full refund and the purchasers were given $1000 of mattel electronics. 
- Intellivoice voice unit (1982) was supported by a few games
- Music synthesizer
- Videoplexer
- 8K ram cart (PlayCable)
- System Changer let you play Atari 2600 games and use atari joysticks
- ECS (1983) tried to change it into a home computer 

### ColecoVision 
Released in 1982 August

Controllers:
- Came with two hard wired 12 button controllers
- Atari 2600 expansion. Let you play Atari 2600 games and user Atari joysticks
- Driving controller: steering wheel and gas pedal
- Adam computer expander: turned the console into an Adam home computer. KB, 64KB RAM, cassette drive, and printer.
- Trackball
- SAC: actual joysticks instead of the normal puck controllers

### Atari 5200 
Released 1982 October
Four controller ports. It was backwards compatible with Atari 2600 games if you bought the adapter (which was not available for the first year of release!). 

Controllers:
- Main controller jumped on the 12 button bandwagon for some reaon. Two fire buttons and analog stick along with select and start buttons. This controller had trouble self centering which made it almost impossible to use for many games. 
- Track ball controller

### Vectrex
Released 1982 November. Unique among home consoles in that it had a built-in vector-graphics CRT, so it did not need a TV. Came with Mine Storm built-in. Discontinued in 1984 after the video game crash.

Controllers:
- Built-in controller in the base of the console (4 buttons + analog stick)
- Second controller sold separately
- 3D Imager (1984 stereoscopic headset, very rare)
- Light pen

## Home computers

### TRS-80 Model 1
1977 August

Sold through Radio Shack stores. Z80 CPU at 1.77 MHz. Spawned a family of business and home models.

Peripherals
- Cassette recorder
- Expansion Interface (parallel printer port, additional RAM, floppy controller)
- 5.25" floppy drives
- RS-232 serial board

### TI-99/4
1979 October

16-bit TMS9900 CPU paired with a video chip (TMS9918) that would later show up in the ColecoVision and MSX. Calculator-style chiclet keyboard.

Peripherals
- Solid State Software cartridges
- Wired remote controllers (joysticks)
- Speech synthesizer module
- Peripheral Expansion Box (memory expansion, floppy controller, RS-232)

### Atari 400
1979 November

Membrane "kid-proof" keyboard, 8K RAM (later 16K), single cartridge slot. Shares the 6502 / ANTIC / CTIA-GTIA / POKEY architecture with the 800.

Peripherals
- Atari 410 cassette drive
- Atari 810 5.25" floppy drive
- Used the same 9-pin DB9 controllers as the 2600 (joysticks, paddles, driving controllers)

### Atari 800
1979 November

Full-travel keyboard, 8K-48K RAM, two cartridge slots, four controller ports. Higher-end sibling of the 400.

Peripherals
- Same drives and controllers as the 400
- Atari 850 Interface Module (RS-232 / parallel)
- 1010 cassette, 810 / 1050 floppy drives

### Sinclair ZX80
1980 February (UK); available in the US via mail order shortly after.

Z80A CPU, 1K RAM, membrane keyboard. The CPU drove the video output directly, so the screen blanked while the machine was computing.

### TRS-80 Color Computer (CoCo)
1980 July

A separate Tandy line from the Model 1, built around the Motorola 6809E rather than the Z80. Not software-compatible with the Model 1 family. Sold through Radio Shack alongside the rest of the TRS-80 line.

Models
| Name   | Date         |
|--------|--------------|
| CoCo 1 | 1980 July    |
| CoCo 2 | 1983 October |

Peripherals
- Cassette and floppy drives
- ROM cartridge slot ("Program Paks")
- Two analog joystick ports (right-angle 5-pin DIN)

### Apple III
1980 November

Intended as a business successor to the Apple II. Famously unreliable at launch (early units had to be "dropped" to reseat chips) and recalled. The Apple III Plus (1983 December) cleaned up most issues but the line was discontinued in 1984.

Peripherals
- Built-in 5.25" floppy drive
- ProFile 5MB hard drive (1981)
- Apple II emulation mode (limited)

### Commodore VIC-20
1981 January (US). Previously sold in Japan as the VIC-1001 starting 1980 October.

MOS 6502, 5K RAM (3.5K user-available), 22-column color display. First computer to sell over one million units.

Peripherals
- Datasette (1530)
- 1540 / 1541 5.25" floppy drives
- VICmodem (1982, $99 - first sub-$100 modem)
- Single DB9 Atari-style joystick port
- Cartridge slot for game and memory expansion

### Sinclair ZX81
1981 March (UK); sold in the US directly by Sinclair starting later in 1981.

Improved successor to the ZX80: same 1K RAM but a "slow" mode that kept the display stable while running BASIC, and a single ULA chip in place of much of the discrete logic.

Peripherals
- 16K RAM pack (notorious for falling out of the edge connector)
- ZX Printer (thermal)

### TI-99/4A
1981 June

Cost-reduced redesign of the 99/4 with a real typewriter-style keyboard and an updated TMS9918A video chip (added bitmap mode). TI exited the home computer market in October 1983 after a destructive price war with Commodore.

Peripherals
- Same as 99/4
- Speech synthesizer (often bundled)

### IBM PC
1981 August (Model 5150)

Intel 8088 at 4.77 MHz, 16K-256K RAM, cassette port + optional 5.25" floppies, MS-DOS / PC-DOS. The reference design the rest of the industry eventually cloned.

Peripherals
- 5.25" floppy drives (160K / 320K SS/DS)
- IBM Monochrome Display Adapter (MDA) or Color Graphics Adapter (CGA)
- IBM Game Control Adapter (analog joysticks, two ports)
- Hercules Graphics Card (third party, 1982)

### Timex Sinclair 1000
1982 July

US-market version of the ZX81, manufactured and distributed by Timex with 2K RAM (up from 1K). At $99.95 it was briefly the cheapest computer in the US and sold over half a million units before being discontinued in 1983.

### Commodore 64
1982 August

64K RAM, SID 6581 sound chip, VIC-II graphics with hardware sprites. Eventually the best-selling single computer model in history.

Peripherals
- Datasette (1530)
- 1541 floppy drive
- 1701 / 1702 composite monitors
- 1600 / 1650 / 1660 modems
- Two DB9 joystick ports
- Cartridge slot

### Atari XL line
1982 / 1983

Slim, cost-reduced redesign of the 400/800 hardware. Mostly software-compatible with 400/800 cartridges and disks (a few timing-sensitive titles broke).

Models
| Name   | Date          |
|--------|---------------|
| 1200XL | 1982 December |
| 600XL  | 1983 June     |
| 800XL  | 1983 June     |

Peripherals
- 1010 cassette, 1050 floppy drive
- Same DB9 controllers as the 2600 / 400 / 800

### Apple Lisa
1983 January

Motorola 68000 at 5 MHz, 1MB RAM, mouse-driven GUI. $9,995 price and slow performance limited sales to a few thousand units. Reworked as the Lisa 2 in 1984 (single 3.5" floppy, ran Macintosh software via MacWorks).

### IBM PC XT
1983 March (Model 5160)

Same 8088 CPU as the PC, but added an internal 10MB hard drive, an extra expansion slot, and dropped the cassette port. Effectively replaced the original 5150 as the standard business PC.

### Mattel Aquarius
1983 June

Z80A-based budget machine marketed by Mattel as a companion to the Intellivision. 4K RAM stock, rubber chiclet keyboard, anemic graphics. Mattel discontinued it within months (1983 October) after poor reception; engineers reportedly nicknamed it "the system for the seventies."

Peripherals
- Mini Expander (added two hand controllers, sound, and a cartridge slot)
- Data Recorder (cassette)
- Thermal printer

### Coleco Adam 
1983 October

Sold either as a standalone computer or as Expansion Module #3 for the ColecoVision. Z80A, 80K RAM, built-in Digital Data Pack tape drive, daisywheel printer. The power supply lived in the printer, so the system would not boot without it. Plagued by production delays and reliability problems; discontinued 1985 January.

Peripherals
- Digital Data Pack tape drives
- Optional 5.25" floppy drive
- 12-button controllers (shared with ColecoVision)
- ColecoVision cartridge compatibility (Super Game cartridges)

### IBM PCjr
1983 November (announced) / 1984 March (shipped) (Model 4860)

Home-market IBM PC. 4.77 MHz 8088, 64K-128K RAM, two cartridge slots, enhanced 16-color graphics and 3-voice sound (the SN76489 also used in the ColecoVision). Infamously launched with a wireless "Chiclet" keyboard that IBM later replaced for free. Discontinued 1985 March.

Peripherals
- Wireless infrared keyboard
- Cartridge slots (unique to the PCjr among IBM PCs)
- Sidecar memory and parallel expansions

### Apple Macintosh
1984 January (introduced with the "1984" Super Bowl ad)

This system will not be available in game. Magazines may mention it though.

Motorola 68000 at 7.83 MHz, 128K RAM, 9-inch 512x342 B&W display, 400K 3.5" floppy, mouse-driven GUI. The 512K "Fat Mac" followed in 1984 September. 16-bit, but pivotal to the era.

Peripherals
- Single-button mouse
- External 400K 3.5" floppy
- ImageWriter dot-matrix printer
- Modem / printer serial ports

### Commodore Plus/4 and 16
1984

These systems will not be available in game. Magazines may mention it though.

The "264 series." Not software-compatible with the C64 (different chipset, no SID, no sprites). The Plus/4 had built-in office software in ROM; the 16 was a low-end 16K version. Both flopped in the US.

Models
| Name             | Date      |
|------------------|-----------|
| Commodore 16     | 1984      |
| Commodore Plus/4 | 1984 June |

Peripherals
- 1531 Datasette (non-standard mini-DIN connector)
- 1551 floppy drive
- Non-standard mini-DIN joystick ports (incompatible with C64 / VIC / Atari sticks)

### IBM PC AT
1984 August (Model 5170)

This system will not be available in game. Magazines may mention it though.

Intel 80286 at 6 MHz (later 8 MHz), 1.2MB 5.25" floppy, 16-bit ISA bus, 20MB hard drive. 16-bit machine; mostly outside the 8-bit hobbyist scope of the rest of this list.

### Tandy 1000
1984 November

This system will not be available in game. Magazines may mention it though.

PC-compatible from Tandy/Radio Shack that cloned the IBM PCjr's enhanced graphics and sound rather than the plain CGA/PC speaker of the IBM PC. The Tandy 1000's TGA / 3-voice sound became the de facto target for PC game audio and graphics into the late 80s after IBM killed the PCjr.
